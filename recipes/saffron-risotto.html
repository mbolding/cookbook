Saffron Risotto with Prawns

Prep: 10 mins Cook: 35 mins Serves: 4

Description

A risotto of luminous golden hue — each grain of Arborio swollen with saffron-perfumed broth and finished with butter, Parmesan, and plump prawns seared just so. The dish requires constant, meditative stirring: a labor which some regard as tedious and others find to possess a certain contemplative charm. The result, in either case, is magnificent.

Ingredients

  • 300g Arborio rice
  • Large pinch saffron threads
  • 1 liter hot fish or chicken stock
  • 200ml dry white wine
  • 300g large prawns, peeled and deveined
  • 1 large shallot, finely diced
  • 3 cloves garlic, minced
  • 2 tbsp olive oil
  • 60g cold butter, cubed
  • 60g Parmesan, finely grated
  • Zest and juice of 1 lemon
  • Salt, white pepper, and a pinch of cayenne
  • Fresh chives, to garnish

Instructions

  1. Steep saffron in the hot stock for 10 minutes. Keep the stock warm over low heat.
  2. Heat olive oil in a wide, heavy pan over medium heat. Cook shallot for 3 minutes until translucent. Add garlic; cook 1 minute.
  3. Add Arborio rice and stir for 2 minutes until each grain is coated and slightly translucent at the edges — the tostatura.
  4. Pour in wine and stir until fully absorbed.
  5. Begin adding the saffron stock one ladleful at a time, stirring frequently and waiting for each addition to be mostly absorbed before adding the next. Continue for 18–20 minutes. The risotto is ready when the rice is al dente and the consistency is flowing, not stiff — it should ripple slowly when the pan is shaken.
  6. Meanwhile, season prawns with salt, pepper, and cayenne. Sear in a hot pan with a slick of oil for 90 seconds per side until pink and just cooked. Set aside.
  7. Remove the risotto from heat. Beat in cold butter and Parmesan vigorously — this is the mantecatura, the step that creates the famous creaminess. Stir in lemon zest and juice.
  8. Spoon into warmed shallow bowls. Arrange the seared prawns on top. Finish with chives, a thread of good olive oil, and a final grating of Parmesan. Serve immediately — risotto, like certain spells, waits for no one.
